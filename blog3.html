Assessing the Current System:
Natural Gas Power Generation in Bahrain

Technical, economic and environmental analysis of gas-fired power

Posted: 27 June 2026  ·  Nouraldeen M Alsharidi

Stage 2 — Current Energy System Assessment

How Natural Gas Power Works — Technical Overview

Bahrain primarily uses Combined Cycle Gas Turbine (CCGT) technology. In a CCGT plant, natural gas is combusted in a gas turbine (Brayton cycle) to generate electricity. The hot exhaust gases then drive a heat recovery steam generator (HRSG), which feeds a steam turbine (Rankine cycle) — hence "combined cycle".

Technical Strengths

  • Despatchability: Can ramp up or down within minutes — critical for meeting peak demand (summer AC loads in Bahrain)
  • High capacity factor (85–92%): Generates power almost continuously, unlike intermittent renewables
  • Proven technology: Decades of operational experience; well-understood maintenance cycle
  • Grid inertia: Spinning generators provide synchronous inertia, stabilising grid frequency (50 Hz in Bahrain)

Critical Issue — Methane Leakage: Even at 2% upstream leakage, natural gas loses most of its climate advantage over coal on a 20-year global warming potential basis. Bahrain's gas infrastructure, aging in parts, may have higher leakage rates.

Economic Analysis

While CCGT has a competitive LCOE today (~USD 0.068/kWh), this figure is heavily dependent on gas import prices, which are subject to geopolitical volatility. Bahrain's energy subsidy bill reflects the hidden cost of maintaining gas dependency.

Stage 2 Assessment — Natural Gas: Technically excellent for grid reliability and peak load management, but environmentally incompatible with SDG 13 goals due to high CO₂ and methane emissions. Economically vulnerable to fuel price shocks and subsidy dependency.
